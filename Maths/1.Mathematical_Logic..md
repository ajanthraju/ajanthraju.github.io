## 1. Core Concepts and Connectives

Mathematical logic translates natural language into formal symbols to remove ambiguity.

| Name | Symbol | Translation | Shortcut / Truth Condition |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Negation** | $\neg P$ | NOT $P$ | Opposite of $P$. |
| **Conjunction** | $P \wedge Q$ | $P$ AND $Q$ | True ONLY if **both** are True. |
| **Disjunction** | $P \vee Q$ | $P$ OR $Q$ | False ONLY if **both** are False. |
| **Implication** | $P \rightarrow Q$ | If $P$ then $Q$ | False ONLY if $T \rightarrow F$ (Broken Promise). |
| **Bi-conditional** | $P \leftrightarrow Q$ | $P$ iff $Q$ | True if $P, Q$ have the **same** value. |

### Implication Deep-Dive ($P \rightarrow Q$)
* **The Promise Analogy:** "If you get 100% ($P$), then I will buy you a car ($Q$)."
  * $T \rightarrow T =$ True (Promise kept).
  * $T \rightarrow F =$ False (Promise broken - THE ONLY FALSE SCENARIO).
  * $F \rightarrow T =$ True (Pity gift, rule not broken).
  * $F \rightarrow F =$ True (Promise kept).

### Logic Variations (Converse, Inverse, Contrapositive)
* **The Converse ($Q \rightarrow P$):** *Swap the items*. (Example: "If the grass is wet, then it rains." - Not logically equivalent).
* **The Inverse ($\neg P \rightarrow \neg Q$):** *Negate both items*, keep original order. (Example: "If it does not rain, grass is not wet." - Not logically equivalent).
* **The Contrapositive ($\neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$):** *Flip the order AND negate both*. 
  * *The Exam Golden Rule:* The *Contrapositive* is ALWAYS mathematically equivalent to the original statement.
  * *Example Problem:* What is the Contrapositive of "If the system crashes, then the alarm sounds"?
  * **Step 1:** Identify $P$ (system crashes) and $Q$ (alarm sounds).
  * **Step 2:** Negate both: $\neg P$ (system does not crash), $\neg Q$ (alarm does not sound).
  * **Step 3:** Swap their order to form $\neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$.
  * **Final Answer:** "If the alarm does not sound, then the system did not crash."

### Essential Identities
The most frequent transformation in competitive exams:
$$P \rightarrow Q \equiv \neg P \vee Q$$

**The Contrapositive:**
$$(P \rightarrow Q) \equiv (\neg Q \rightarrow \neg P)$$

**De Morgan's Laws:**
$$\neg (P \wedge Q) \equiv \neg P \vee \neg Q$$
$$\neg (P \vee Q) \equiv \neg P \wedge \neg Q$$

## 2. Rules of Inference

Inference rules allow us to move from **Premises** to a **Conclusion** without constructing a full truth table.

1.  **Modus Ponens:** If $P \rightarrow Q$ and $P$ are true, then $Q$ is true.
2.  **Modus Tollens:** If $P \rightarrow Q$ and $\neg Q$ are true, then $\neg P$ is true.
3.  **Hypothetical Syllogism:** If $P \rightarrow Q$ and $Q \rightarrow R$, then $P \rightarrow R$.
4.  **Disjunctive Syllogism:** If $P \vee Q$ and $\neg P$, then $Q$.
5.  **Constructive Dilemma:** If $(P \rightarrow Q) \wedge (R \rightarrow S)$ and $(P \vee R)$, then $(Q \vee S)$.

## 3. First-Order Logic (FOL) & Quantifiers

FOL uses quantifiers to define the scope of a variable.

* **Universal ($\forall x$):** "For every $x$". Usually paired with Implication ($\rightarrow$).
    * *Example:* $\forall x (S(x) \rightarrow L(x))$ (All students have laptops).
* **Existential ($\exists x$):** "There exists some $x$". Usually paired with Conjunction ($\wedge$).
    * *Example:* $\exists x (S(x) \wedge L(x))$ (Some students have laptops).

### De Morgan's Laws for Quantifiers
When you push a negation ($\neg$) inside or outside a quantified statement, the quantifier flips.
1. **Negating Universal:** $\neg \forall x P(x) \equiv \exists x \neg P(x)$ 
   *(If not everyone is P, then someone is not P).*
2. **Negating Existential:** $\neg \exists x P(x) \equiv \forall x \neg P(x)$
   *(If there does not exist anyone who is P, then everyone is not P).*

### Nested Quantifiers
The order of different quantifiers changes the entire meaning of the system:
* $\forall x \exists y P(x, y)$: "For every person $x$, there is someone $y$ they love." (Everyone loves *somebody*).
* $\exists y \forall x P(x, y)$: "There is a specific person $y$ that everyone $x$ loves." (There is one universal idol).
* *Rule:* You can swap identical quantifiers ($\forall x \forall y \equiv \forall y \forall x$), but you **cannot** swap mixed quantifiers without changing the logic.

## 4. Normal Forms in Propositional Logic

In computing and logic design, we standardize boolean expressions into specific formats to easily test for satisfiability or tautology. 

* **Literal:** A variable or its negation (e.g., $P$ or $\neg P$).
* **Minterm:** An `AND` ($\wedge$) product of all variables in the system. (Used in DNF).
* **Maxterm:** An `OR` ($\vee$) sum of all variables in the system. (Used in CNF).

### Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF)
A formula is in DNF if it is a **Sum of Products** (an OR of ANDs). 
* **Format:** $(A \wedge B) \vee (C \wedge \neg D) \vee E$
* **Principal DNF:** Also called Canonical DNF. It is the sum of **Minterms**. Every individual clause must contain exactly one instance of every variable (either normal or negated).
* **Database Analogy:** Checking for specific, exact rows: `WHERE (Age=30 AND Dept='IT') OR (Age=25 AND Dept='HR')`.

### Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)
A formula is in CNF if it is a **Product of Sums** (an AND of ORs).
* **Format:** $(A \vee B) \wedge (C \vee \neg D) \wedge E$
* **Principal CNF:** Also called Canonical CNF. It is the product of **Maxterms**. 
* **Database Analogy:** Broad filtering query: `WHERE (Status='Active' OR Role='Admin') AND (Location='NY' OR Location='CA')`.

### Conversion Rules
To convert any expression to CNF or DNF:
1. Remove implications ($P \rightarrow Q \equiv \neg P \vee Q$).
2. Push negations inward using De Morgan's Laws.
3. Distribute $\vee$ over $\wedge$ (for CNF) or distribute $\wedge$ over $\vee$ (for DNF).

## 5. Advanced Relations: POSETs, Lattices & Partitions

### Partially Ordered Sets (POSETs)
A relation $R$ on a set $S$ is a Partial Order if it is:
1. **Reflexive** ($a \leq a$)
2. **Anti-symmetric** (If $a \leq b$ and $b \leq a$, then $a = b$)
3. **Transitive** (If $a \leq b$ and $b \leq c$, then $a \leq c$)
* *Example:* The "Subset of" ($\subseteq$) relation or "Divides" ($|$).

### Hasse Diagrams
A visual representation of a POSET where redundant arrows are removed.
* **Rules for drawing:**
  1. Remove all self-loops (Reflexivity is implied).
  2. Remove all transitive arrows (If A points to B, and B to C, delete the arrow from A to C).
  3. Draw it directionally from bottom to top, so arrowheads can be removed.

### Lattices
A POSET is a **Lattice** if *every* pair of elements has both:
1. **Least Upper Bound (LUB) / Supremum:** The smallest element that both point up to.
2. **Greatest Lower Bound (GLB) / Infimum:** The largest element that points up to both.
* *Trap:* If even one pair of elements in the Hasse Diagram lacks a unique LUB or GLB, the structure is NOT a lattice.

### Partitions and Equivalence Classes
An **Equivalence Relation** (Reflexive, Symmetric, Transitive) breaks a set into completely isolated, non-overlapping groups called **Equivalence Classes**. 
* The collection of all these classes forms a **Partition** of the set.
* **Rules of a Partition:**
  1. No class is empty.
  2. The intersection of any two different classes is $\emptyset$ (They are mutually exclusive).
  3. The union of all classes exactly equals the original master set.

## 6. Deconstructed Practice Problems (1–18)

### Problem 1: Negation of Implication
* **Question:** Simplify $\neg (P \rightarrow Q)$.
* **Deconstruction:** Using the identity $P \rightarrow Q \equiv \neg P \vee Q$, we get $\neg (\neg P \vee Q)$. Applying De Morgan's Law, the negation flips $\neg P$ to $P$, $\vee$ to $\wedge$, and $Q$ to $\neg Q$.
* **Solution:** $P \wedge \neg Q$.

### Problem 2: Logical Equivalence
* **Question:** What is logically equivalent to "If I study hard ($P$), then I will pass ($Q$)"?
* **Deconstruction:** Only the **Contrapositive** is logically equivalent. This requires swapping the terms and negating both.
* **Solution:** $\neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$.

### Problem 3: FOL Translation
* **Question:** Translate "Every student in the class has a laptop."
* **Deconstruction:** "Every" implies $\forall$. In FOL, universal statements usually take the form "If $x$ is a student, then $x$ has a laptop."
* **Solution:** $\forall x (S(x) \rightarrow L(x))$.

### Problem 4: "Only If"
* **Question:** Translate "Internet ($I$) only if Subscriber ($S$)."
* **Deconstruction:** "Only if" identifies a **Necessary** condition, which goes on the right side of the arrow.
* **Solution:** $I \rightarrow S$.

### Problem 5: Nested Quantifiers
* **Question:** Translate $\forall x \exists y L(x, y)$ ($x$ loves $y$).
* **Deconstruction:** Read left to right. "For every person $x$, there is at least one person $y$ that $x$ loves."
* **Solution:** "Everybody loves somebody."

### Problem 6: Tautology Check (Inference)
* **Question:** Is $((P \rightarrow Q) \wedge \neg Q) \rightarrow \neg P$ a tautology?
* **Deconstruction:** This is the symbolic form of **Modus Tollens**. If we try to make it false by setting the conclusion $\neg P$ to False ($P=T$), the premise $((T \rightarrow Q) \wedge \neg Q)$ becomes False regardless of $Q$.
* **Solution:** Yes, it is a Tautology.

### Problem 7: "Unless" Logic
* **Question:** Translate "System fails ($F$) unless backup kicks in ($B$)."
* **Deconstruction:** "A unless B" means "If not B, then A" ($\neg B \rightarrow A$) OR "If not A, then B" ($\neg A \rightarrow B$).
* **Solution:** $\neg B \rightarrow A$ (also equivalent to $B \vee F$).

### Problem 8: Constructive Dilemma
* **Question:** Given $P \rightarrow Q, R \rightarrow S$, and $P \vee R$.
* **Deconstruction:** Since we have either $P$ or $R$ (the antecedents), we are guaranteed to get either result $Q$ or $S$ (the consequents).
* **Solution:** $Q \vee S$.

### Problem 9: Negation of Quantifiers
* **Question:** Negate $\forall x (P(x) \rightarrow Q(x))$.
* **Deconstruction:** $\neg \forall$ becomes $\exists$. The negation of $(P \rightarrow Q)$ is $(P \wedge \neg Q)$.
* **Solution:** $\exists x (P(x) \wedge \neg Q(x))$.

### Problem 10: False Hunting
* **Question:** Is $P \rightarrow (Q \rightarrow P)$ a Tautology?
* **Deconstruction:** Try to make it False. Set the result $(Q \rightarrow P)$ to False ($Q=T, P=F$). Now check the start: $P \rightarrow \dots$. Since $P=F$, the whole implication is automatically True. It can never be False.
* **Solution:** Yes, it is a Tautology.

### Problem 11: Necessary vs Sufficient
* **Question:** If $Square \rightarrow Rectangle$, what is NOT necessarily true?
* **Deconstruction:** In $P \rightarrow Q$, $P$ is sufficient and $Q$ is necessary. Reversing them ($Q \rightarrow P$) is the "Converse" and is not guaranteed.
* **Solution:** "Being a rectangle is sufficient for being a square" ($R \rightarrow S$ is false).

### Problem 12: FOL Identity
* **Question:** What is logically equivalent to $\neg \exists x (P(x) \wedge Q(x))$?
* **Deconstruction:** Negate the $\exists$ to get $\forall$. Negate the inside to get $\neg P(x) \vee \neg Q(x)$. This is the OR-form of $P(x) \rightarrow \neg Q(x)$.
* **Solution:** $\forall x (P(x) \rightarrow \neg Q(x))$.

### Problem 13: Duality Principle
* **Question:** Find the Dual of $(P \wedge Q) \vee \neg P$.
* **Deconstruction:** To find the dual, swap $\wedge$ for $\vee$ and $\vee$ for $\wedge$. Leave $\neg P$ alone (do not negate variables).
* **Solution:** $(P \vee Q) \wedge \neg P$.

### Problem 14: Bi-conditional
* **Question:** What is equivalent to $(P \rightarrow Q) \wedge (Q \rightarrow P)$?
* **Deconstruction:** This is the exact definition of a two-way implication (Bi-conditional).
* **Solution:** $P \leftrightarrow Q$.

### Problem 15: Universal Instantiation
* **Question:** Given $\forall x (P(x) \rightarrow Q(x))$ and $P(a)$ is true for a specific element $a$.
* **Deconstruction:** What is universally true for the whole set is true for a specific member $a$. Since $P(a)$ is true, $Q(a)$ must follow via Modus Ponens.
* **Solution:** $Q(a)$.

### Problem 16: CNF Conversion
* **Question:** Convert $(P \rightarrow Q) \rightarrow R$ to CNF.
* **Deconstruction:** Convert to ORs: $\neg (\neg P \vee Q) \vee R$. Apply De Morgan's: $(P \wedge \neg Q) \vee R$. To make it CNF (Product of Sums), distribute the $R$: $(P \vee R) \wedge (\neg Q \vee R)$.
* **Solution:** $(P \vee R) \wedge (\neg Q \vee R)$.

### Problem 17: Satisfiability
* **Question:** Is $(P \vee Q) \wedge (\neg P \vee \neg Q)$ satisfiable?
* **Deconstruction:** This is the boolean XOR gate. It is true if $P=T, Q=F$ or vice versa. Since at least one row in the truth table is True, it is satisfiable.
* **Solution:** Yes.

### Problem 18: Functional Completeness
* **Question:** Which sets of operators are functionally complete?
* **Deconstruction:** A set is complete if it can derive all three fundamental operations: $\{\neg, \wedge, \vee\}$. Both $\{\neg, \vee\}$ (NOR logic) and $\{\neg, \rightarrow\}$ can do this.
* **Solution:** Both $\{\neg, \vee\}$ and $\{\neg, \rightarrow\}$.

## 7. Summary Table for Exams

| Term | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Tautology** | Always True under all conditions. |
| **Contradiction** | Always False under all conditions. |
| **Satisfiable** | True in at least one case (at least one row in truth table is T). |
| **Contingency** | Neither a Tautology nor a Contradiction (mixed T's and F's). |
| **Valid Argument** | The Conclusion is guaranteed True whenever all Premises are True. |
