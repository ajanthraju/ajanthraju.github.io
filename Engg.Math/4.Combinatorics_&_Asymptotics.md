## Permutations vs. Combinations
* **Permutations (Order Matters):** Use when the exact sequence changes the outcome. 
  $$^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$
  * *Example:* 3-letter passwords from A, B, C, D, E. Order matters (ABC $\neq$ CBA). $5 \times 4 \times 3 = 60$ passwords.

* **Combinations (Order Does NOT Matter):** Use when the internal sequence is irrelevant.
  $$^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$$
  * *Example:* 3-person committees from 5 people. Person A and Person B is the same as Person B and Person A. $\frac{5!}{3! \times 2!} = 10$ committees.

* **The AND / OR Rule:** * If choosing Group A *AND* Group B, *Multiply* combinations.
  * If choosing Group A *OR* Group B, *Add* combinations.

## The Pigeonhole Principle
* **The Rule:** If you have $N$ items to put into $K$ boxes, and $N > K$, at least one box MUST contain more than one item. (Analogy: 13 people guarantee two share one of the 12 birth months).

## Generating Functions & Recurrence Relations
* **Generating Functions:** Encodes an infinite sequence of numbers as the *coefficients of a polynomial*.
* **Recurrence Relations:** Defines the next number based on previous numbers.
  * *Example Problem:* Find the roots of the recurrence relation $a_n = 7a_{n-1} - 10a_{n-2}$.
  * **Step 1:** Replace terms with $r$ and set the equation to zero: 
    * $a_n \rightarrow r^2$
    * $a_{n-1} \rightarrow r^1$
    * $a_{n-2} \rightarrow$ *Only Coefficient* (no $r$ term)
    * *The resulting equation:*  $$r^2 - 7r + 10 = 0$$
  * **Step 2:** Now, find the roots of the quadratic equation:  $$(r - 5)(r - 2) = 0$$
  * **Step 3:** The roots are **$r_1 = 5$** and **$r_2 = 2$**.

## Asymptotics (Big-O Notation)
* **Big-O ($O$):** *Worst-case scenario* (Upper Bound).
* **Big-Omega ($\Omega$):** *Best-case scenario* (Lower Bound).
* **Big-Theta ($\Theta$):** *Exact Average Case* (Tight Bound).
* **Finding the Bound:** Always drop constants and lower-order terms. *(Example: For f(n) = 5n<sup>2</sup> + 2n + 1, the highest power is n<sup>2</sup>, so it is O(n<sup>2</sup>)).*
* **Speed Ranking (Fastest to Slowest):** O(1) < O(log n) < O(n) < O(n log n) < O(n<sup>2</sup>) < O(2<sup>n</sup>).

### Practice Questions (Solved)

1. How many ways can you arrange the letters in the word "COMPUTER"?
**Answer:** 8! *(8 distinct letters permutation: 8 x 7 x 6 ...)*

2. In a group of 6 boys and 4 girls, in how many ways can a committee of 4 members be formed if it must consist of exactly 2 boys and 2 girls?
**Answer:** 6C<sub>2</sub> x 4C<sub>2</sub>. *(Combinations multiplied because of "AND").*

3. What is the minimum number of people required in a room to guarantee that at least two people share the exact same birth month?
**Answer:** 13.

4. At a business meeting, 10 people shake hands with every other person exactly once. How many total handshakes occur?
**Answer:** 45. *(10C<sub>2</sub>. A handshake is a combination of 2 people).*

5. Which asymptotic notation is used to describe the Worst-Case running time of an algorithm?
**Answer:** Big-O (O).

6. Let f(n) = 5n<sup>2</sup> + 2n + 1. What is the strictly tightest Big-O notation for this function?
**Answer:** O(n<sup>2</sup>).

7. If an algorithm requires a minimum of n log n operations to complete in the best possible scenario, this is represented by:
**Answer:** Big-Omega (Ω(n log n)).

8. Arrange the following time complexities from fastest to slowest execution time: $O(n^2), O(1), O(n \log n), O(n)$.
**Answer:** $O(1) < O(n) < O(n \log n) < O(n^2)$.
