## MODULE 1: PERMUTATIONS VS. COMBINATIONS

### Key Concepts & Real-World Examples
* **PERMUTATIONS (Order Matters):** * *Math Rule:* nPr = n! / (n-r)!
  * *Everyday Analogy:* A padlock with a number dial. If the code is 1-2-3, entering 3-2-1 will not open the lock. The specific sequence is strictly enforced.
* **COMBINATIONS (Order Does NOT Matter):** * *Math Rule:* nCr = n! / [r! * (n-r)!]
  * *Everyday Analogy:* Making a fruit smoothie. If you put in Strawberries, Bananas, and Apples, it yields the exact same smoothie as putting in Apples, Bananas, and Strawberries. The membership matters, but the order is irrelevant.
* **THE "AND / OR" COUNTING RULE:** * If a selection requires choosing Group A **AND** Group B, **Multiply** the combinations.
  * If a selection requires choosing Group A **OR** Group B, **Add** the combinations.

## MODULE 2: THE PIGEONHOLE PRINCIPLE

### Key Concepts & Real-World Examples
* **The Rule:** If you have N items to put into K boxes, and N > K, at least one box MUST contain more than one item.
* *Everyday Analogy:* You have a drawer full of loose black socks and white socks in the dark. How many socks do you need to pull out to guarantee a matching pair? **Three.** There are only 2 colors (boxes), so if you pull 3 socks (pigeons), at least two of them must be the same color.

## MODULE 3: ASYMPTOTICS (ALGORITHM GROWTH BOUNDS)

### Key Concepts & Real-World Examples
This measures the speed and efficiency of an algorithm as the data size (n) scales toward infinity. When analyzing equations, always drop constants and lower-order terms to find the dominant highest power.

* **BIG-O (O) - The Upper Bound (Worst-Case Scenario):**
  * *Everyday Analogy:* Packing an umbrella when traveling. It represents the absolute maximum rain you might face. Your algorithm will *never* take longer than this limit.
* **BIG-OMEGA (Ω) - The Lower Bound (Best-Case Scenario):**
  * *Everyday Analogy:* The absolute fastest time you could possibly drive to work if you hit every single green light with zero traffic. Your algorithm will *never* be faster than this.
* **BIG-THETA (Θ) - The Tight Bound (Exact Average Case):**
  * *Everyday Analogy:* Your standard, everyday commute time. It accurately tracks the actual time it takes, bounding it closely from both the top and bottom.
* **GROWTH RANKING (Fastest to Slowest):** * O(1) < O(log n) < O(n) < O(n log n) < O(n²) < O(2ⁿ)

## DAILY VERIFICATION Q&A (PYQ BANK)

**Q1. (GATE CS) How many ways can you arrange the letters in the word "COMPUTER"?**
* **Answer:** 8!
* **Logic:** There are 8 distinct letters. Arranging all of them where order matters is a permutation of 8 items: 8 * 7 * 6 * 5 * 4 * 3 * 2 * 1 = 8!

**Q2. (ISRO IT) In a group of 6 boys and 4 girls, a committee of 4 members is to be formed. In how many ways can this be done if it must consist of exactly 2 boys AND 2 girls?**
* **Answer:** 6C2 * 4C2
* **Logic:** Use combinations because the order of people on a committee doesn't matter. Because you need boys AND girls, you multiply their respective combinations.

**Q3. (GATE CS) What is the minimum number of people required in a room to guarantee that at least two people share the exact same birth month?**
* **Answer:** 13
* **Logic:** By the Pigeonhole Principle, there are 12 possible months (boxes). To guarantee a collision, you need 12 + 1 = 13 people (pigeons).

**Q4. (TRB) At a business meeting, 10 people shake hands with every other person exactly once. How many total handshakes occur?**
* **Answer:** 45
* **Logic:** A handshake requires choosing 2 people from a pool of 10, where order doesn't matter (A shaking B is the same as B shaking A). Therefore, 10C2 = (10 * 9) / (2 * 1) = 45.

**Q5. (GATE CS) Let f(n) = 5n² + 2n + 1. What is the strictly tightest Big-O notation for this function?**
* **Answer:** O(n²)
* **Logic:** Drop the constants (5 and 1) and drop the lower-order term (2n). The dominant highest power dictates the worst-case time complexity.

**Q6. (ISRO CS) If an algorithm requires a minimum of n log n operations to complete in the best possible scenario, this is represented by:**
* **Answer:** Ω(n log n)
* **Logic:** The "best-case" lower bound is mathematically represented by Big-Omega (Ω).
