## Propositional Logic Operators
* **Negation ($\neg$):** Opposite value.
* **Conjunction (AND / $\wedge$):** True only if *both* statements are True.
* **Disjunction (OR / $\vee$):** True if *at least one* statement is True.
* **Implication (IF-THEN / $\rightarrow$):** Evaluates to False *ONLY* when a True premise leads to a False conclusion ($T \rightarrow F = F$).
  * *The Promise Analogy:* "If you get 100% ($P$), then I will buy you a car ($Q$)."
  * $T \rightarrow T =$ True (Promise kept).
  * $T \rightarrow F =$ False (Promise broken - THE ONLY FALSE SCENARIO).
  * $F \rightarrow T =$ True (Pity gift, rule not broken).
  * $F \rightarrow F =$ True (Promise kept).
* **Bi-conditional ($\leftrightarrow$):** True if $P$ and $Q$ have the same truth value.

## Logic Variations (Converse, Inverse, Contrapositive)
* **The Converse ($Q \rightarrow P$):** *Swap the items*. (Example: "If the grass is wet, then it rains." - Not logically equivalent).
* **The Inverse ($\neg P \rightarrow \neg Q$):** *Negate both items*, keep original order. (Example: "If it does not rain, grass is not wet." - Not logically equivalent).
* **The Contrapositive ($\neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$):** *Flip the order AND negate both*. 
  * *The Exam Golden Rule:* The *Contrapositive* is ALWAYS mathematically equivalent to the original statement.
  * *Example Problem:* What is the Contrapositive of "If the system crashes, then the alarm sounds"?
  * **Step 1:** Identify $P$ (system crashes) and $Q$ (alarm sounds).
  * **Step 2:** Negate both: $\neg P$ (system does not crash), $\neg Q$ (alarm does not sound).
  * **Step 3:** Swap their order to form $\neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$.
  * **Final Answer:** "If the alarm does not sound, then the system did not crash."

## Logical Equivalences
* **De Morgan's Laws:** Distributing a negation ($\neg$) *flips the internal operators*. 
  * $\neg(P \vee Q) \equiv \neg P \wedge \neg Q$

## Tautology and Contradiction
* **Tautology:** A proposition that is *always True*. Example: $P \vee \neg P$ (It will rain or it will not rain).
* **Contradiction:** A proposition that is *always False*. Example: $P \wedge \neg P$ (I am tall AND I am not tall).

## First-Order Logic (Quantifiers)
* **Universal ($\forall x$):** *"For all x"*. Property holds for all elements in the domain.
* **Existential ($\exists x$):** *"There exists an x"*. Property holds for at least one element in the domain.
* **Negating Quantifiers:** To disprove "All P are Q", you must find *"At least one P that is NOT Q"*.
  * *Example Problem:* Negate $\forall x (P(x) \rightarrow Q(x))$.
  * **Step 1:** Flip $\forall$ to $\exists$.
  * **Step 2:** The implication ($P \rightarrow Q$) is logically ($\neg P \vee Q$).
  * **Step 3:** Negating ($\neg P \vee Q$) using De Morgan's gives ($P \wedge \neg Q$).
  * **Final Answer:** $\exists x (P(x) \wedge \neg Q(x))$.

Numerical Example: Truth Table Construction
To prove $(P \land Q) \rightarrow P$ is a Tautology:

| $P$ | $Q$ | $P \land Q$ | $(P \land Q) \rightarrow P$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| T | T | T | T |
| T | F | F | T |
| F | T | F | T |
| F | F | F | T |

*Since all outputs are True, it is a Tautology.*

## Practice Questions (Solved)
1. Which of the following is the truth value of (P → Q) when P is False and Q is True?
**Answer:** True. *(Only True → False evaluates to False).*

2. According to De Morgan's Law, the expression ¬(P ∨ Q) is logically equivalent to:
**Answer:** ¬P ∧ ¬Q.

3. The compound proposition P ∨ ¬(P ∧ Q) is a:
**Answer:** Tautology. *(Expands to P ∨ ¬P ∨ ¬Q, which is always True).*

4. What is the negation of the statement: "All data engineers use SQL" (∀x, P(x))?
**Answer:** There exists at least one data engineer who does not use SQL.

5. If P is True and Q is False, what is the truth value of (¬P ∨ Q)?
**Answer:** False. *(¬True ∨ False becomes False ∨ False).*

6. Is $\neg(P \wedge Q)$ equivalent to ($\neg P \vee \neg Q$)?
**Answer:** Yes. *(This is De Morgan's Law)*.

7. Is ($P \wedge \neg P$) a Tautology or a Contradiction?
**Answer:** Contradiction.

8. Negate the statement: $\forall x (P(x) \rightarrow Q(x))$.
**Answer:** $\exists x (P(x) \wedge \neg Q(x))$.
