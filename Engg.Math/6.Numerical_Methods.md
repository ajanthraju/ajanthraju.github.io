## 1. Visualizing Equations & Finding Roots
A "root" is simply the exact mathematical point where a graph crosses the horizontal x-axis (meaning y = 0).

### Linear Equations (Systems and Intersections)
* **The Equation:** y = mx + b (Forms a perfectly straight line).
* **Finding the Root (Formula):** Set y to 0 and isolate x. 

<pre>
       -b
x = --------
        m
</pre>

* **The Mental Image:** When you have a "System of Linear Equations", you are just drawing two or more lines on the exact same piece of paper.
  * **Unique Solution:** The lines cross at exactly one point (an intersection).
  * **No Solution:** The lines are perfectly parallel. They will never cross.
  * **Infinite Solutions:** The lines are the exact same line drawn on top of each other.

### Quadratic Equations (Parabolas and Roots)
* **The Equation:** y = ax² + bx + c. That squared term makes the line curve into a "U" shape called a parabola.
* **Finding the Roots (Formula):** Use the standard quadratic formula to find where the U-shape hits the floor:

<pre>
      -b ± √(b² - 4ac)
x = --------------------
             2a
</pre>

* **The Mental Image:** * **2 Real Roots:** The "U" dips below the floor and comes back up, crossing the floor twice.
  * **1 Real Root:** The very bottom tip of the "U" perfectly rests on the floor, touching it exactly once.
  * **0 Real Roots (Imaginary):** The entire "U" is floating in the air above the floor. It never touches the x-axis.

## 2. Core Concept: Numerical Methods
"Standard" math is how humans solve problems (like using algebra to isolate x). "Numerical Methods" is how **computers** solve problems. Computers are terrible at algebra, but they are incredibly fast at guessing. Numerical Methods are just mathematically structured "guessing algorithms" that get closer and closer to the exact answer until the error is basically zero.

### Topic A: LU Decomposition (Solving Systems of Equations)
* **The Human Way:** If you have 3 variables (x, y, z) and 3 equations, you use substitution or calculate a massive Inverse Matrix.
* **The Computer Way:** Calculating an Inverse Matrix takes too much processing power. Instead, the algorithm breaks a complicated square matrix (A) into two simpler pieces: a Lower triangular matrix (L) and an Upper triangular matrix (U). Therefore, **A = LU**.
* **The Analogy:** Imagine trying to exactly split a $143.76 restaurant bill among 7 people. That is hard (Matrix A). LU Decomposition is like breaking that bill down into $100, $40, and $3.76 pieces first. It makes the final division much faster and less prone to errors.
* **Visualizing the Matrix Split:** Here is exactly what the computer sees. Notice the big block of 0s in the top-right of L, and the bottom-left of U.

<pre>
[ a  b  c ]       [ L11   0     0  ]       [ U11  U12  U13 ]
[ d  e  f ]   =   [ L21  L22    0  ]   x   [  0   U22  U23 ]
[ g  h  i ]       [ L31  L32  L33  ]       [  0    0   U33 ]
</pre>

### Topic B: Root-Finding Algorithms (Solving Non-Linear Equations)
**1. The Bisection Method (Safe, but Slow)**
* **The Logic:** The "Higher/Lower" guessing game. Find one point where y is positive, and one point where y is negative. You know the graph MUST cross the zero line between them. You guess the exact middle, chopping the window in half over and over.
* **The Formula:** <pre>
               a + b
New Guess = -----------
                 2
</pre>

* **Exam Trait:** It is guaranteed to eventually find the answer (it always converges), but it takes a lot of steps.

**2. The Newton-Raphson Method (Fast, but Risky)**
* **The Logic:** Pick a random starting point. Draw a straight line matching the slope (tangent) of that point, and slide down that line straight to the x-axis. Your next guess is where that line hits the axis.
* **The Formula:** <pre>
                                f(x_current)
x_next  =  x_current  -  -------------------------
                               f'(x_current)
</pre>

* **The Exam Trap:** It is incredibly fast. BUT, it fails completely if the derivative f'(x) is exactly zero, because you cannot divide by zero. This happens if your guess lands exactly on the flat peak or valley of a curve.

**3. The Secant Method**
* **The Logic:** A compromise. Similar to Newton-Raphson, but it does not require you to calculate the actual derivative. Instead, it uses a secant line drawn between two previous guesses.

### Topic C: Numerical Integration (Finding Area Under a Curve)
Integration calculates the total area underneath a wavy line. Computers do this by slicing the area into easy-to-measure shapes.

**The Mental Image (Fencing a Curvy Hill):** Imagine building a fence up against a curvy hill.
* **The Trapezoidal Rule:** You only have *straight* wooden planks. You connect straight planks across the posts. Because the hill curves, your straight plank will poke into the dirt or leave an air gap. Decent estimate, but slightly inaccurate.
* **Simpson's 1/3 Rule:** You have *flexible, bendy* plastic sheets. You bend the sheet across *three* posts at a time (creating a parabola). It hugs the curve perfectly, making it highly accurate.

**1. The Trapezoidal Rule Formula**
* **The Formula:** <pre>
          h
Area &approx;  ---  * [ y_0 + y_n + 2(y_1 + y_2 + ... + y_n-1) ]
          2
</pre>

* **Exam Shortcut:** Multiply height (h) by 1/2. The first and last terms are single walls. Every intermediate wall is multiplied by 2 because it is shared by the trapezoid next to it.

**2. Simpson's 1/3 Rule Formula**
* **The Strict Constraint:** Because Simpson's bendy sheet requires exactly three posts to form its curve, it spans two fence panels at a time. Therefore, the total number of sub-intervals (n) **MUST be an even number**. If n is odd, the rule cannot be used.
* **The Formula:** <pre>
          h
Area &approx;  ---  * [ y_0 + y_n + 4(Odd Terms) + 2(Even Terms) ]
          3
</pre>

* **Exam Shortcut:** Multiply height (h) by 1/3. The first and last terms are single. Multiply all odd-indexed terms by 4, and all even-indexed terms by 2.

## Daily Verification (Practice Questions)

1. **(TRB Model)** Which of the following root-finding methods will completely fail if the derivative (slope) of your initial guess evaluates exactly to zero?
**Answer:** C) Newton-Raphson Method.

2. **(GATE IT)** In Numerical Integration, what is the strict prerequisite constraint for applying Simpson's 1/3 Rule to estimate the area under a curve?
**Answer:** C) The number of sub-intervals (n) must be an even number.
