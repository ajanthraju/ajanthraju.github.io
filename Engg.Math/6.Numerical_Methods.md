## 1. Visualizing Equations & Finding Roots
A **"root"** is simply the exact mathematical point where a graph crosses the horizontal x-axis (meaning $y = 0$).



[Image of x-intercept on a coordinate plane]


### Linear Equations (Systems and Intersections)
* **The Equation:** $y = mx + b$ (Forms a perfectly straight line).
* **Finding the Root:** Set $y$ to $0$ and isolate $x$.
* **The Formula:** $$x = -\frac{b}{m}$$
* **The Mental Image:**
  * **Unique Solution:** The lines cross at exactly one point.
  * **No Solution:** The lines are perfectly parallel.
  * **Infinite Solutions:** The lines are the exact same line drawn on top of each other.



[Image of intersecting, parallel, and coincident lines]


### Quadratic Equations (Parabolas and Roots)
* **The Equation:** $y = ax^2 + bx + c$. The squared term curves the line into a "U" shape called a **parabola**.
* **Finding the Roots:** Use the quadratic formula to find where the U-shape hits the "floor":
$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$
* **The Mental Image:**
  * **2 Real Roots:** The "U" dips below the x-axis and crosses it twice.
  * **1 Real Root:** The tip of the "U" rests perfectly on the x-axis.
  * **0 Real Roots:** The "U" floats in the air, never touching the axis.





## 2. Core Concept: Numerical Methods
"Standard" math is how humans solve problems (algebra). **Numerical Methods** is how computers solve problems via structured "guessing algorithms" that get closer to the answer until the error is near zero.

### Topic A: LU Decomposition
The computer breaks a complicated square matrix ($A$) into two simpler pieces: a **Lower** triangular matrix ($L$) and an **Upper** triangular matrix ($U$).

**The Split ($A = LU$):**

$$
\begin{bmatrix} 
a & b & c \\ 
d & e & f \\ 
g & h & i 
\end{bmatrix} = 
\begin{bmatrix} 
L_{11} & 0 & 0 \\ 
L_{21} & L_{22} & 0 \\ 
L_{31} & L_{32} & L_{33} 
\end{bmatrix} \times 
\begin{bmatrix} 
U_{11} & U_{12} & U_{13} \\ 
0 & U_{22} & U_{23} \\ 
0 & 0 & U_{33} 
\end{bmatrix}
$$



### Topic B: Root-Finding Algorithms

**1. The Bisection Method (Safe, but Slow)**
Find one positive $y$ and one negative $y$; the root must be between them. Keep chopping the window in half.
$$x_{new} = \frac{a + b}{2}$$

**2. Newton-Raphson Method (Fast, but Risky)**
Slide down a **tangent line** from your guess to the x-axis. 
* **The Formula:** $$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$
* **The Trap:** Fails if the derivative $f'(x_n)$ is zero (a flat peak or valley).



**3. The Secant Method**
A compromise that uses a line connecting **two** previous guesses. It doesn't require Calculus (no derivatives needed).



### Topic C: Numerical Integration (Area Under a Curve)

**1. The Trapezoidal Rule**
Connects points with straight lines (trapezoids).
* **The Formula:** $$\text{Area} \approx \frac{h}{2} [y_0 + y_n + 2(y_1 + y_2 + \dots + y_{n-1})]$$
* **Shortcut:** First and last walls are single; all intermediate walls are doubled.



**2. Simpson's 1/3 Rule**
Uses parabolas to hug the curve. Highly accurate.
* **The Strict Constraint:** The number of sub-intervals ($n$) **must be an even number**.
* **The Formula:** $$\text{Area} \approx \frac{h}{3} [y_0 + y_n + 4(\sum y_{odd}) + 2(\sum y_{even})]$$





## Daily Verification (Practice Questions)

1. **Which method fails if the derivative of your guess is zero?**
   * **Answer:** Newton-Raphson Method.
2. **Prerequisite for Simpson's 1/3 Rule?**
   * **Answer:** The number of sub-intervals ($n$) must be an **even number**.

   * **Answer:** All intermediate ordinates (excluding the first and last).
