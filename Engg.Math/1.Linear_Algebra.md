## Matrix Properties & Eigenvalues
* **The Trace:** The sum of the elements on the *main diagonal*. The *Trace* is always equal to the *Sum of the Eigenvalues*.
* **The Determinant:** A number representing how much a matrix *scales an area or volume*. The *Determinant* is always equal to the *Product of the Eigenvalues*.
* *Example:* If a $3 \times 3$ matrix has eigenvalues of $1, 2,$ and $5$, the Determinant is $1 \times 2 \times 5 = 10$.
* **Singular Matrix:** A matrix with a determinant of exactly $0$. It loses its volume and has *no Inverse*.

## Matrix Operations (Addition & Multiplication)
### 1. Matrix Addition 
* **The Golden Rule:** You can ONLY add matrices together if they have the exact same dimensions (e.g., you can add a $2 \times 2$ to a $2 \times 2$, but you cannot add a $2 \times 2$ to a $3 \times 3$).
* **The Method:** Simply add the numbers that are in the exact same positions.
```
[ 1  2 ]   +   [ 5  6 ]   =   [ 1+5  2+6 ]   =   [  6   8 ]
[ 3  4 ]       [ 7  8 ]       [ 3+7  4+8 ]       [ 10  12 ]
```
### 2. Matrix Multiplication (Row $\times$ Column)
* **The Golden Rule:** To multiply Matrix $A \times$ Matrix $B$, the number of columns in A MUST equal the number of rows in B.
* **Exam Trick:** Write their dimensions side-by-side: $(2 \times 3)$ and $(3 \times 2)$. If the two inside numbers match, you can multiply them. The two outside numbers tell you the size of your final answer (a $2 \times 2$).
* **The Method (The "Dive and Sweep"):** You multiply the Rows of the first matrix by the Columns of the second matrix, and add the products together to get a single number.

## The Vocabulary: Conjugate & Adjugate
### The Conjugate Matrix
This only matters if your matrix contains Complex Numbers (numbers with an imaginary $i$, like $3 + 4i$).
* **The Definition:** To find the conjugate of a matrix, you simply go to every single cell and flip the positive/negative sign of the imaginary part ($i$).
* **Example:** If a cell contains $3 + 4i$, its conjugate is $3 - 4i$. If a cell contains just a normal real number like $7$, it stays $7$.
* **Exam Context:** If an exam gives you a matrix of purely normal, real numbers, its conjugate is just the exact same matrix.

### The Adjugate Matrix (The Adjoint)
The Adjugate is the heavy-lifting part of the formula to find the Inverse of a matrix. 
* **The Definition:** The Adjugate is the Transpose of the Cofactor matrix.
* **The $2 \times 2$ Exam Shortcut:** To avoid long math for $2 \times 2$ matrices:
  1. Swap the two numbers on the main diagonal (top-left to bottom-right).
  2. Flip the positive/negative signs of the other two numbers.
  *Boom. You just found the Adjugate Matrix.*

## The Inverse of a Matrix ($A^{-1}$)
* **The Core Concept:** The inverse cancels out the original matrix, leaving the *Identity matrix* ($A \cdot A^{-1} = I$). A matrix ONLY has an inverse if its Determinant is NOT zero.
* **The Formula:** $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\text{Determinant}} \times \text{Adjugate}$.

## Triangular & Orthogonal Matrices (Exam Shortcuts)
* **Triangular Matrix:** A square matrix where all entries below (or above) the main diagonal are *exactly zero*.
  * *Shortcut:* The determinant is simply the *product of the main diagonal numbers*. The eigenvalues *ARE* the main diagonal numbers.
* **Orthogonal Matrix:** A matrix where multiplying it by its own *Transpose* gives the *Identity matrix* ($A \cdot A^T = I$).
  * *Shortcut:* The Transpose is the exact same thing as the Inverse ($A^T = A^{-1}$). Its determinant is always exactly $+1$ or $-1$.
* **Transpose Product Rule:** The transpose of two multiplied matrices *reverses their order*: $(AB)^T = B^T A^T$.

## The Cayley-Hamilton Theorem
* **The Core Rule:** Every square matrix satisfies its own *characteristic equation*. Replace $\lambda$ with the Matrix ($A$), and numbers with the Identity Matrix ($I$).
* **The Inverse Shortcut:** How to find $A^{-1}$ without heavy calculation:
  * **Step 1:** Write the characteristic equation (e.g., $A^2 - 4A + 3I = 0$).
  * **Step 2:** Multiply the entire equation by $A^{-1}$ to drop the powers: $A - 4I + 3A^{-1} = 0$.
  * **Step 3:** Use basic algebra to isolate the $A^{-1}$ term: $3A^{-1} = 4I - A$.
  * **Step 4:** Divide to get the Final Answer: $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{3}(4I - A)$.

## Rank of a Matrix
* **The Definition:** The rank is the number of completely *unique, non-zero rows* of information. Redundant rows (exact multiples) or blank rows drop the rank.
  * *Single Zero Row:* A $3 \times 3$ matrix with two data rows and one row of $[0, 0, 0]$ has a Rank of $2$.
  * *Two Zero Rows:* A $3 \times 3$ matrix with one data row and two rows of $[0, 0, 0]$ has a Rank of $1$.
  * *Null Matrix:* A matrix of entirely zeros has a Rank of $0$.

## Systems of Linear Equations
* **No Solution (Inconsistent):** $Rank(A) \neq Rank([A|B])$. (Analogy: Parallel train tracks).
* **Infinite Solutions (Redundant):** $Rank(A) = Rank([A|B]) < Variables$. (Analogy: Two roads painted on top of each other).
* **Unique Solution (Perfect Match):** $Rank(A) = Rank([A|B]) = Variables$. (Analogy: Roads meeting at one intersection).

## Practice Questions (Solved)
1. The trace of a $3 \times 3$ matrix is $12$ and its determinant is $36$. If two of the eigenvalues are $2$ and $3$, what is the third eigenvalue?
**Answer:** 6. *(The determinant is the product of eigenvalues: 2 x 3 x X = 36).*

2. If a $2 \times 2$ matrix $A$ satisfies the equation $A^2 - 5A - 2I = 0$, what is the expression for $A^{-1}$?
**Answer:** 1/2(A - 5I). *(Multiply by A<sup>-1</sup> to get A - 5I - 2A<sup>-1</sup> = 0, then solve).*

4. What is the rank of a $3 \times 3$ matrix where Row 1 is $[1, 2, 3]$, Row 2 is $[2, 4, 6]$, and Row 3 is $[0, 0, 0]$?
**Answer:** Rank $1$. *(Row 2 is a multiple of Row 1, and Row 3 is zero)*.

5. A system of equations $Ax = B$ is consistent and has a unique solution if:
**Answer:** $Rank(A) = Rank([A|B]) = \text{number of variables}$.

6. If a $3 \times 3$ matrix has eigenvalues $1, 2,$ and $5$, what is its Determinant?
**Answer:** $10$. *(1 x 2 x 5 = 10)*.

7. What is the rank of a matrix where all $3$ rows are identical and non-zero?
**Answer:** Rank $1$. *(Identical rows are redundant)*.

8. If the Rank of $A = 2$ and the Rank of $[A|B] = 3$ for a system with $3$ variables, how many solutions exist?
**Answer:** $0$ (No solution). *(The ranks do not match)*.
